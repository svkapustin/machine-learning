Implementation: Outlier Detection¶

Detecting outliers in the data is extremely important in the data preprocessing step of any analysis. The presence of outliers can often skew results which take into consideration these data points. There are many "rules of thumb" for what constitutes an outlier in a dataset. Here, we will use Tukey's Method for identfying outliers: An outlier step is calculated as 1.5 times the interquartile range (IQR). A data point with a feature that is beyond an outlier step outside of the IQR for that feature is considered abnormal.

In the code block below, you will need to implement the following:

  • Assign the value of the 25th percentile for the given feature to Q1. Use np.percentile for this.
  • Assign the value of the 75th percentile for the given feature to Q3. Again, use np.percentile.
  • Assign the calculation of an outlier step for the given feature to step.
  • Optionally remove data points from the dataset by adding indices to the outliers list.

NOTE: If you choose to remove any outliers, ensure that the sample data does not contain any of these points!
Once you have performed this implementation, the dataset will be stored in the variable good_data.

Additionally, if a signifiant amount of variance is explained by only two or three dimensions, the reduced data can be visualized afterwards.

The silhouette coefficient for a data point measures how similar it is to its assigned cluster from -1 (dissimilar) to 1 (similar). Question 10¶

Companies will often run A/B tests when making small changes to their products or services to determine whether making that change will affect its customers positively or negatively. The wholesale distributor is considering changing its delivery service from currently 5 days a week to 3 days a week. However, the distributor will only make this change in delivery service for customers that react positively. How can the wholesale distributor use the customer segments to determine which customers, if any, would react positively to the change in delivery service?
Hint: Can we assume the change affects all customers equally? How can we determine which group of customers it affects the most?

Intuition may tell us that the change in delivery service will not affect all customers equally. Food service business will be affected most negatively, because they want to serve fresh products. Grocery shops or markets will likely be ok with every-second day delivery service because the products they carry have much longer shelf life such as detergents, paper, etc. To justify our suspicion, and obtain more scientific evidence, we need to conduct a controlled experiment such as A/B test.

A/B test

The goal of this test is to find a way to increase profits by reducing expenses. One of the distributor's high-cost expenses is a delivery service. When switched from 5-day per week morning service to 3-day per week evening service, the company will save, and thus increase the profit.

Previous experiment with limited proper planning resulted in negative effect on the profits. Some of the affected customers canceled deliveries because of the unacceptable new delivery schedule.

We need to create more selective criteria as to which customers would be accepting of the change in delivery, but leave the existing schedule in place for those customers that are unable to switch.

We assume that at present, with 5-day delivery service, the customers raise no complains (alternatively, we extract the mean/median number of complains and use it as a base case). We also anticipate that with the change to delivery service, some subset of customers will raise complains. Therefore, to measure the effect of our changes, we want to monitor for the number of complains and thus designate it as an experimental variable.

To conduct the test we randomly select a subset of existing customers from each segment in equal proportion, but exclude the outliers so as not to affect the major clients while conducting the experiment. The selected subset is our test group. The remaining customers are our control group for which no changes are made for the test duration.

We switch the delivery service to 3 days per week for the test group and start collecting the complains. The complains are assigned to the corresponding customer for further analysis.

Upon test completion, we measure the magnitude of the complains for each segment of the test group and compare it to the corresponding values of the control group segments.

If the number of complains exceeds some threshold/significance level for a given segment, the distributor should find alternative solutions in attempts to reduce operating costs.
